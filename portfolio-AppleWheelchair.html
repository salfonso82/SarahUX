Improving Accessible Navigation in Apple Maps

For Travel-limiting individuals are people with temporary or permanent physical or cognitive conditions that make traveling outside the home more difficult. In the United States, millions of adults experience mobility-related barriers that reduce their ability to travel independently, access employment, or rely on personal vehicles.

With my project I sought to explore how Apple Maps could better support wheelchair and mobility users by enabling accessible, door-to-door trip planning. The goal is to improve user confidence, trust, and independence for people with limited mobility and their caregivers during complex travel scenarios.

Discovery Stage

For many individuals with limited mobility live in urban areas due to closer proximity to healthcare services and higher baseline accessibility. However, through early research and observation, I found that accessibility challenges are often more pronounced in smaller towns, where infrastructure updates and accommodations depend heavily on local advocacy and resources.To balance these differences, I focused my research on suburban environments, which typically fall between high- and low-accessibility contexts.

To better understand how users plan, adapt, and navigate trips with confidence, I conducted interviews with individuals with limited mobility and their caretakers. Interview questions focused on local travel habits, pre-trip planning, navigation challenges, and accessibility-related obstacles encountered in daily life.

Research and Findings

Interviews revealed that accessibility failures pose greater barriers to travel than distance or traffic. Users navigate trips while balancing safety, physical fatigue, and medical needs, making unexpected obstacles especially disruptive.

Mobility needs vary widely, creating demand for layered accessibility details beyond basic “wheelchair accessible” labels. Participants also valued a combination of crowdsourced and official updates to support both pre-trip planning and real-time navigation. These insights suggest that improved accessibility information can increase confidence, independence, and participation for users and caregivers alike.

Competative Analysis

Apple Maps Competative Analysis based on research

While this project aims to create a unique experience for Apple Maps, stakeholder expectations require solutions that are reliable and proven. To identify established patterns, I reviewed accessibility-related features in Google Maps and Waze, Apple Maps’ primary competitors.

Google Maps places greater emphasis on pre-planning accessibility, offering features such as wheelchair-friendly transit options, step-free routes, accessibility labels, photos, and terrain indicators. These features help users and caregivers anticipate potential challenges before leaving home. Many of these insights are supported by user contributions and accessibility-focused data.

Waze, in contrast, prioritizes real-time, community-driven reporting, enabling users to share live updates about traffic, road conditions, and disruptions. While less focused on accessibility, its reporting system demonstrates how collective input can improve navigation confidence and trust.

These observations informed design decisions by balancing user-validated accessibility needs with proven navigation patterns, ensuring proposed features remain both practical and scalable within Apple Maps.

Define Stage

Personas

After completing interviews and research, I was able to narrow down 3 levels of low mobility users:

  • Low Mobility with wheelchair(manual and motorized)
  • Wheelchair with caregiver (elderly)
  • Higher mobility but limited limb use

Based on these personas and my previously noted insights, I then focused on further narrowing down my focus statements for the later part of my program:

  • How might we transform Apple Maps from a directions tool into an accessibility-first journey companion that makes travel predictable, safe, and dignified for people with limited mobility and their caregivers?
  • How might we allow users to set and reuse accessibility preferences that reflect different mobility levels, equipment types, and caregiver needs?
  • How might we ensure that familiar locations/route information is updated so users anticipate delays? (similar to official updates on NYC subways)(Crowdsourced updates from nearby users)
